### Start with two separate questions

First confirm that your visa permits the activity you plan to perform. Then analyze tax separately. A visa category controls immigration status; it does not automatically determine whether you are a Korean tax resident or whether a particular payment is taxable.

### Review residency and income sourcing

Korean tax residency can depend on domicile, family, occupation, assets, expected stay, and time in Korea. The familiar 183-day threshold is important, but it is not a complete test. A non-resident can still have Korean tax on Korean-source income.

For remote work, document where the services were physically performed, who directs the work, which entity pays the salary, and where the payment is received. Do not label salary as purely foreign just because the employer and bank account are outside Korea.

### Check short-term resident and treaty rules

Foreign residents whose total period of domicile or residence in Korea is five years or less during the previous ten years may be taxed differently on some foreign-source income depending on payment or remittance facts. This rule is fact-sensitive and should not be reduced to a slogan.

If two countries may treat you as resident or tax the same salary, review the applicable treaty. Tie-breaker rules, employment-income articles, and foreign tax credits can interact.

### Build a defensible record

Keep a travel-day calendar, Korean address history, employment contract, pay statements, bank records, and proof of foreign tax. Revisit the position before a long-stay milestone, a major remittance, a contract change, or the May filing season.
